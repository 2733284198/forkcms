Martin Scorsese has been acknowledged as the greatest American filmmaker of his generation.

Martin Scorsese (Wikipedia)

The evidence of this critical acclaim is abundant: Mean Streets (1973), Taxi Driver (1976) and Raging Bull (1980) are all listed amongst the 360 Great Films as selected by Sight and Sound and

the British Film Institute; Raging Bull was selected as the greatest American film of the 1980s by American Film; and Raging Bull finished second in the voting of Top Ten Films of all time (behind Citizen Kane) in an international poll of filmmakers in Sight and Sound in 1992.

His lack of recognition from the Academy Awards actually adds to rather than detracts from his reputation; after all, Orson Welles, Alfred Hitchcock and Stanley Kubrick were also all denied Oscars. With this acclaim by both filmmakers and critics alike, Scorsese has been able to accumulate a great deal of cultural prestige and power, despite the limited success and at times outright failure of his films at the box office.

Scorsese´s prestige also comes from his championing of film preservation and his efforts to ensure our "film heritage" is not lost.
